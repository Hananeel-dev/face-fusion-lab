How the composite is made

The detector estimates facial landmarks for both photos, crops each face, rotates the crop so the eyes are level, and resizes it to a shared 256 by 256 frame.

Both faces are then warped with a Delaunay triangle mesh so their eyes, nose, mouth, jawline, and face boundary move toward the average landmark shape.

The final image is a 50/50 pixel blend of the two warped faces. Inputs are not retained by the service; only the generated output image is written for display.

What the score means

The score is a visual similarity estimate for compositing, not an identity-verification result. It tells you how easily the two faces align and blend.

Landmark geometry compares normalized face structure. Warp similarity measures how much deformation was needed. Texture similarity compares brightness/detail after alignment. Composite agreement measures remaining pixel-level variation.

Higher scores usually mean the people have more similar pose, face proportions, and image texture. Lighting, blur, expression, camera angle, and glasses can move the score.
